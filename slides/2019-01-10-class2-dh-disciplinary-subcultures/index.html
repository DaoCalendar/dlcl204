Digital Humanities Across Borders

Class 2: DH and disciplinary (sub-)cultures

Why use digital tools and methods?

Makes it (sometimes much) faster to answer research questions you'd be asking otherwise.

Gives you greater precision in quantifying common knowledge, which facilitates comparisons.

(Your mileage may vary in how people respond to this.)

Lets you answer research questions that would be impossible in analog form.

Gives you a community during the often isolating experience of getting a PhD (big DH & smaller disciplinary-DH.

(Also alt-ac & non-academic job prospects, etc.)

Can make it easier to come up with a research question you want to pursue.

Text markup

  • Good for representing what you know about a text in a computationally tractable way
    • You might have come to know those things in a completely "non-digital" way
  • Possibility of many different kinds of outputs to accommodate different kinds of research questions

Great multilingual flexibility... but lots of manual work in marking up the text and creating outputs.

Thematic research collections

Counting words

  • What are the most frequent words are in a corpus?
  • How do they differ between works in an author's oeuvre?
  • What kind of lexicon do different characters use in a work?
  • How does translation change the lexical patterns of the source text?
  • Is there a significant difference in the lexica used by male and female authors?
  • How does word use reveal patterns of class, race, and gender privilege?

Simple tools for counting "words" assume the language has word morphology like English.

Counting certain kinds of words

(Stylistics, "literary syntax")

A how-to

  1. Identify grammatical features that have consistent meaning in a given text or group of texts, and can be plausibly related to some condition of the text (such as authorship and genre) or the production environment of the text (history, media change).
  2. Confirm that these features are not attributable to chance and are significant in relation to a corpus of similar texts. “Significance” and “similar” will both depend on the critical project. 

graph by Craig Messner

Stylometry

  • How might an author’s individual background and past experience (not) influence her/his literary production?
  • Investigations of a "human ‘stylome’" -- analogous to human genome,  depicting measurable relationship between identity and style (authorship studies)
  • Looking at collaborative forms of authorship, investigating the role of editors, scribes or secretaries, or authorship within the context of translation studies
  • "Stylochronometry": correlations between a text’s writing style and its (absolute or relative) date of composition

Topic modeling

 "Theme analysis is a critical approach in which an observed pattern is documented and explored in order to reveal its—or perhaps more accurately, to imbue it with—explanatory power." (Ross & El Khatib)

"What exactly is a topic? Formally, a topic is a probability distribution over terms. In each topic, different sets of terms have high probability, and we typically visualize the topics by listing those sets." (Blei)

  • Archival exploratory tool
  • "Literary theoretical springboard"
    • Seeing how themes fail to cohere w/o close reading (Rhody)
  • Questions about whether these topics are actually coherent, stable, meaningful (Schmidt)

Word vectors

  • Visualization of lexical space
  • Looking at shifting meanings of individual words
  • Extracting social networks within literature (Wohlgenannt, Chernyak, Ilvovsky 2016)
  • Discovery through analogy (e.g. leading textbook assigned for a given discipline)

(Geo-)spatial tools

  • Can we reveal spatial segregations of plot lines, characters, that are otherwise difficult to elicit from the text?
